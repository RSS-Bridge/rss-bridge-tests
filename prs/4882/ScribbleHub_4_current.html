Chapter 1: The Leftovers Club

Jacob Ballast liked to think of a billiards table as a tiny world.

He had never been good at anything else. No sports, no instruments, and certainly not school. He’d scraped by in everything he needed to. There had been classes that had been better — math — and classes that had been worse — English — but college had never sparked an interest beyond the one thing he could do.

When his parents had split, a nine year old Jacob had been given his first taste of billiards. His father had rented a new place, and the landlord had a pool table in the basement. For a nine year old who’s life had ripped to shreds, it had been perfect. A tiny world. His tiny world. Yes, only made of felt, but one that could not be disturbed by anyone else. He played everyday. Hours and hours in the basement, knocking balls into pockets, aligning everything in his brain to play it perfectly.

It always stayed in that basement. His public school didn’t have anything resembling a club for billiards. As far as anyone in his family could tell, the most organized games near them took place in bars.

So, by the time Jacob had gone to college, it was only really a hobby of the past. Something he did as a kid. The one thing he was good at, but the one thing that no one else cared about. To be good at pool was like being good at juggling — a pretty party trick, but something that no one ever paid or asked for.

Except, of course, that it was a fantastic party trick.

“What the fuck.” Christine was leaning on a pool cue, dumbfounded. “I mean, what the fuck.”

“I like pool,” said Jacob, setting his cue back on the table. There was another set of people waiting. The bar, Tipsy’s, was overcrowded, everyone looking for a drink, a dance, or a cue.

“That’s nuts.”

“My dad had a table when I was a kid.”

“I just want to repeat for you, that was nuts.” Christine set her own cue down, and the table was immediately swarmed. The pair of them backed into the wall.

Tipsy’s was not their usual haunt. The three of them — Daisy was already back at the booth, nursing a drink and pouring over a book — liked to be places quieter. Low light was fine, but the loud music and the thumping crowd was not their standard. They left that in college, back when their friend group was larger and more prone to seeing sunrise.

“I like pool.”

Christine gestured towards the bar, and they started over. “Yeah, I like pool, too. I just didn’t know you were that good. I didn’t know anyone was that good.” They shoved through a couple who had monopolized the lane to the bartender. “Sorry!” Then, once they’d reached the bar, Christine shook her head. “You literally spun the ball.”

“It’s not that hard,” said Jacob.

“It looked pretty hard,” said Christine. “In fact, I was entirely unable to do anything like that.”

“You’re not very good at pool.”

“Good at pool and perceptive. It’s a wonder you’re single.”

The bartender caught their eye and slowly made his way down the bar towards them. After ordering, Jacob shifted sideways, allowing Christine to slide into the space next to him. She was shorter than just about anyone he’d ever met, which meant that when she slipped in, his chin was almost resting on top of her bouncing, black hair. “Being good at pool is not very good for dating.”

“I don’t know,” said Christine. “If I met a guy who could play pool like you, I’d be more than impressed. There’s something about competence, you know?”

“You mean a guy other than me who could play pool like me,” said Jacob.

“Yes, obviously.” The bartender returned, and Christine grabbed the drinks before Jacob could. “Last name is Moreno.” The bartender nodded, and Christine started off towards the table. When they reached it, again having to weave between and past a disturbing number of guys in skinny jeans, Christine added, as if it hadn’t been a full minute since they’d last said a word, “The Leftovers Club has very stern rules.” She slid down into the booth, right next to Daisy. “Replenishments.”

Daisy didn’t look up from her book. She remained bent over it, her black hair drooping onto the laminated pages.

Since the day Jacob had met her, Daisy had wanted nothing more than to be a doctor. Never mind that she didn’t come for money, and never mind anything else. Med school or bust. When she’d transitioned, mid way through their junior year, there had been only a week where she had seemed even slightly out of sorts. Then, it had been back to studying.

Now, past the MCATs, she spent all her time making sure she never lost her scholarship, and more time making sure she could pay rent with whatever time that remained. Christine and Jacob had long accepted that the only chance to hang out with Daisy would be if they convinced her she could study while they drank.

“The rules of the Leftovers Club are very stern,” agreed Daisy, still trained on the book.

“I didn’t say I wanted to date anyone,” said Jacob.

“You just tried to woo me with your extremely impressive pool skills,” said Christine.

“Jacob has extremely impressive pool skills?”

“Like a shark. Daisy, he spun the ball.”

Jacob almost laughed when Daisy looked up from her book. Beer, friends, not rewards comparable to learning that Jacob might actually be good at something. “I did not try to woo anyone,” he said. “Being good at things is not wooing.”

“Competence is a kind of wooing,” said Daisy, returning to her book.

“If that was true, there’d be a dozen people in this booth watching you study while half drunk.” Jacob took a sip of his own beer.

“I shooed them all away before you showed.”

Christine leaned forward. “Have you ever thought about bringing a date to a pool hall? I mean, seriously, you could do the whole thing where you stand behind them and show them how to get the ball in the hole.”

“As a fact,” said Jacob, “I have not considered doing the most stereotypical thing imaginable.”

“It’d work,” said Christine.

“It would not work,” said Jacob.

Christine waved her beer over the table. “Debatable.”

“Look,” said Jacob. “It’d feel weird to play pool for a date.”

“Because it would be too easy,” said Christine.

“Because it’s not what pools about.”

That got another raise of Daisy’s head. “What is pool about?” she asked.

“I don’t know,” said Jacob. It felt a stupid thing to think about. But it felt wrong to use it to pick up someone. He’d always enjoyed it for it’s simplicity. There was nothing outside the game that could infiltrate, nothing that could interrupt the flow. It seemed sacrilegious to use it for a date. “It’s just wrong.”

Daisy laughed. “Holy shit, how good are you?”

“Daisy, I only had one turn,” said Christine. “Swear to God.”

“It was a good night,” said Jacob.

“Fuck me,” said Daisy, closing her book. “Alright, I want to see too.”

They waited twenty minutes before the table opened again. This time, the bar had rotated to doubles. It left Daisy and Jacob as the challengers. Challenging, it seemed, a pair of men who had held the table for a few rounds.

There was a kind of pleasure Jacob got out of winning. It would be wrong to call his life particularly hard; at twenty four, everything had gone mostly according to plan. He’d gone to a decent college, gotten a decent degree, and ended the transition period, the one of tumult for everyone, with a stable enough life.

But he’d never quite been good at anything, and to be good at something, even if it just was a party trick, was worth relishing. Daisy and Christine had always impressed him. Daisy was whip smart, on her way to medical school, and Christine had been introduced to him as a gymnast. They had been so apparently skilled. And, despite his desire to find something other than the most bar-trick game of all time, Jacob had only ever just been Jacob.

He’d always taken the tact that it was better not to reveal pool as a skill until it came up. Then, at least, it wouldn’t make him sound so washed up. It wouldn’t sound like he took pride in it.

When he lined up to break, though, he did take a little. The cue felt nice between his fingers. Light wood, settled right into the groove that he could make with his fingers. Such a simple thing, and yet so easy to fuck with. Sometimes, on Sundays, they’d call football a ‘game of inches’. Pool was millimeters. Hairs breadth. Slightly off, and the break was wrong, the ball hit the pocket’s edge, the spin was off.

It felt good to know in his bones the right spot to let the cue settle.

He broke, and left the guys who had won the games before with nothing to work off. Pocket one stripe, or one solid, but nothing after.

“Was that good?” whispered Daisy into his ear.

Jacob grinned at her. “It was fine.”

“What do I do?” she asked.

“They’ll probably pick stripes. So you’ll want to go for a solid.”

Daisy leaned on him, watching one of the guys, who was sporting a truly terrific beard, start lining up a shot. “Is there pool strategy?”

“Only if you’re really good at pool.”

“So there is pool strategy, then.”

“Daisy, I think you should focus on just getting a ball into a pocket.” The bearded man sank his shot. Jacob and Daisy would be solids. “You’ll probably want to give that blue one by the corner a go. It’ll probably be free.”

It would not have been Jacob’s first choice; it was the easy shot, but it also made the next shot more difficult. In pool, if you made a shot, you got to take the next one. It meant that at a certain point, you began thinking two or three or even four shots ahead. Jacob had always enjoyed the turn between thinking about where the target ball would go and cue ball. Each mattered of near equal importance, but no one who played only casually knew that.

Still, Daisy ought to have a go at making a shot first. This was a bar, not a competition.

The bearded man missed his next shot.

“Alright,” said Daisy. “Alright.”

“Don’t sweat it too much,” said Jacob.

“Why, because you’re going to finish the job no matter what?” asked Daisy.

“Maybe.”

Daisy rolled her eyes and started chalking her cue. She ended up at the far side from Jacob, right in front of the bearded man and his friend. The bearded man smirked and whispered something into his friend’s ear.

Daisy took her shot. It was piss poor, bouncing off the wall, missing the seven she had been aiming for entirely. If Jacob hadn’t whispered the instruction in her ear, he wasn’t sure he would have guessed which ball she was aiming for at all.

Behind her, the two guys exchanged a look that screamed of course.

If Daisy noticed, she ignored them. She came back around the table to stand next to Jacob. “Sorry.”

“Don’t sweat it,” said Jacob. He watched the bearded man roll around the table, picking his spot. “Would you be mad if I beat them on the next time around?”

“Jake, I came to watch you play pool,” said Daisy, grinning at him. “Besides, I really do need to study.”

The bearded man sank his shot. Then, he sank his next one.

Problem was, the man did no planning at all. As soon as he made the second ball, he pulled up, scanning the table. He’d left himself not a single make-able shot, and barely anything that might allow him to avoid scratching.

When Jacob wanted it to, the table could unfurl before him. Less like a game, and more like a line of dominoes. He could press one, tick it off, and watch every ball fall into place. On and on and on they could go, and all he had to do is make sure that he did not interrupt the chain. Keep it moving, keep it all falling, keep it all perfect as it needed to be.

Six, then four, then the five, and then he went on to the two, which was easily the hardest shot in his little rotation, and by the time he’d bounced the ball in off the far wall, it settling nicely into the far corner pocket, the table was starting to look a lot clearer. From there, he chanced a glance at the two men. No smirks. He chanced a glance at Daisy too, who was doing a very bad job at hiding her smile. When she saw him looking, she said, “You’re a freak.”

Jacob snorted, then finished his sequence. When he pocketed the eight ball, he looked back to the two guys and set his cue on the table. “You can keep it.”

“Nah,” said the bearded guy, shaking his head. “That was crazy.”

“Thanks,” said Jacob.

Daisy set her cue down, too, pointed towards the booth where they’d left Christine, and started off. “Okay, so, were you a professional?”

“I was a very sad child,” said Jacob, solemnly.

“Funny.”

They slipped back through the crowd, then back into the booth. When they arrived, Christine was waiting with new drinks and raised eyebrows.

“So,” she asked, “was it not kind of hot?”

“It was indeed kind of hot,” said Daisy, sliding back down the booth and flipping open her text book. “You should bring dates.”

“I’m not bringing dates.”

“Too bad.” Christine slumped back in the booth, eying the pool table. “We should have had you place bets with the people at the table. You could have won, like, a lot of money.”

Jacob snorted, flipping to get a look at the players. The two they’d beat had stayed on. He couldn’t see, but he was pretty sure that the bearded man was about to miss whatever shot he took. That, or Jacob was just being hopeful. “I’m not going to hustle anyone.”

“Who said anything about hustling?” asked Christine. “I’m not asking you to pretend to be shit. I just think you could get some money out of those guys.”

“Not true,” murmured Daisy, face still buried in her book. “Wrong look.”

“Oh,” said Christine, leaning back and considering him. “That’s true. That’s true, you do look too much like a shark.”

Jacob had never quite considered that he looked much like anything, but a shark had certainly not been on the cards. “I do not look like a shark.”

“Listen, now that I’m over the shock of you being able to levitate the balls into the pockets—”

“It’s not levitation.”

Christine carried on as if she hadn’t heard him. “I can see it, you know. You just look like the kind of guy who would be good at pool.”

“Oh, I didn’t realize there was a stereotype.”

“Sure,” said Christine. She gestured out at him, generally, at nothing in particular, and Jacob laughed. “It’s just all there.”

“So much so that you were shocked to find out that I played pool,” said Jacob.

“But think of the professionals,” said Christine. “They would know, you know? Like, I bet those guys at the table knew that you were good at pool just from looking at you. You’re too…” She trailed off, fingers fluttering in the air. It was Daisy who finished the sentence.

“Wesley Snipes,” she said.

“Exactly,” agreed Christine.

“Well, I’ll be sure to pick out my best Woody Harrelson outfit when I decide to become a hustler,” said Jacob. “Or, you know, not.”

“You just look like you belong,” said Daisy. She stuck a finger in the book, finally looked up, and grabbed her beer. “Do you guys want to move tables? Or, like, maybe out of the bar.” She gestured over her shoulder, towards a swell of noise and laughter. “They’re just a little too loud.”

They shifted down three tables. Then, after a half hour, when Daisy was still unable to study any longer, and Christine and Jacob had both sufficiently drank their way through the boredom of another night in Madison, they left. Out into a wet November, rain drizzling slowly through the streetlight.

Jacob had always liked Madison, at least enough to stay. Almost everyone else he had known had left. Minneapolis, Chicago, or further. Dozens of friends spread across time zones and states and borders, leaving him to scramble to rebuild a life. Even still, there was something warm about living somewhere you had lived all your adult life. Washed, maybe, but cozy, too. He’d always admired the people who had gone from cradle to grave in a single place, loving and living in the shadow of the same main street with the same sidewalks and the same street lamps, outlasting each and every diner and deli and department store.

He’d chosen Madison. Not because of the perfect deli, or the perfect streets, or even the other people who stayed. He liked Christine and Daisy, but it had mostly been a convenience that they became friends. Madison was a choice. Maison was home because he needed to pick a home, and he’d always decided that choosing was the only way that he could make a home at all. It had never just developed as a kid, sprung from the heart and love of his parents. He’d been forced to find it. And so, when he’d graduated college, and he’d looked around and seen no job far away, and no job in his old hometown, and no job in Madison, he’d picked here. To try, at least.

They walked east, down along the empty sidewalk until it became only shoulder, all the while Christine complaining about the rain. They had each stayed for school — Christine for her Masters, Daisy for her MD. None of them had been particularly close, only friends on the periphery of each other, but they had come together because there was no one else. The Leftovers Club, Christine had branded them. Jacob was sure that she had picked it up from a Stephen King adaptation.

It helped to have people to lean back on. Choosing to make Madison home had not quite made it home, not completely. Choosing them, even if they were choices of circumstance, made it feel a little more so. He worried a little about the day they might graduate and go, but not on nights like this. Not on nights when he had the rain on his neck and the soft warmth of beer in his belly.

“Same time Tuesday?” he asked as they approached Christine’s.

“It’d be trivia night,” said Christine. “Someone would need to put the book down.”

“You guys would be fine without me,” said Daisy. “Like, literally fine.”

“Not true,” replied Christine and Jacob in unison.

Daisy hopped up onto the curb, then stretched a hand out to one side for balance. “You two are plenty smart.”

“You’re going to be a doctor, Daisy,” said Christine. “A doctor.”

“Maybe.”

“Maybe they’ll have a medical category and you include it in your study quota,” said Jacob. “It’s like a work outing.”

“Fine,” said Daisy, her hand still out.

They reached the intersection to Christine’s. She said goodbye, hugged them both, and then disappeared down the street, walking faster and faster than before. Then, Daisy and Jacob carried on, down the increasingly cracked road, under the increasingly yellowing streetlights.

And the dimming of the streetlights didn’t stop Daisy from wobbling down the curb, arm out and steady. She carried on with complete precision, her arm never wavering.

“Speaking of secret skills,” said Jacob, when she hopped over a little crack.

Daisy smiled and tilted her head back, face speckled in shadow. “It’s even less practical than yours.”

“There are curbs everywhere,” offered Jacob.

“So true,” agreed Daisy.

They walked for another minute in quiet, the light flickering in and out, the cars missing from the street. It was only when they started getting close to Jacob’s turn off — him towards the little room in the little house that he had managed to rent, her towards the housing for graduate students — that Daisy hopped off the curb and said, “Were you actually a sad kid?”

“What?”

“In the bar,” said Daisy. “After we finished playing, you said you were a sad kid.”

“Oh,” said Jacob. It felt a little wrong to call himself a sad child. Everything about years one through eighteen had been mostly fine. There had been no major deaths, no family members disappearing, no ruthless bullies or dead puppies or evil principals. Yes, his parents’ divorce had been a kind of traumatic, and he wasn’t on the best terms with his father, but they were the kind of issues that were rather pathetic in the grand scheme of things.

So, after a beat, he said, “Kind of. Just, you know, the way that all kids are. And my dad was weird about video games, so when I was at his house, I played a lot of pool.”

“Sure,” said Daisy.

“I didn’t need extensive therapy or anything.”

“Well, as long as you didn’t need extensive therapy.”

The fork was marked by a great tree, one that seemed more trunk than living thing. As a student, Jacob had smoked weed behind it, because it was far enough from the student housing that no one would bother them, and swirling branches obscured them from any patroling police. Here, the pair started to divide. Unlike Christine, they never hugged.

“Tuesday,” said Jacob, almost just to remind her.

“Yeah.”

“It’ll be good to have a night off from studying,” he said.

“Goodnight, shark.”

And then, like he so often was, Jacob was alone.

He didn’t mind it. When Jacob was little, he had often found himself, as all kids were, in brief eras of obsession. And one of those obsessions had been with Apollo 11, the mission that had landed Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin on the moon. The entire mission had fascinated him. The intricacies, the grandiosity of it, but the small parts too. And his favorite small part, the one that had gripped him from the ages of ten to eleven, was with the third astronaut: Michael Collins, who had never landed on the moon and had circled it from above, waiting in the quiet of space.

Michael Collins had never been lonely. Jacob had read it, although he couldn’t remember where. It, above everything else, had taken a hold on every corner of his imagination. An adult, doing incredible things, happy to watch and see the others go on, take the glory, who could circle the moon to the point where no one on earth could speak to him, and still be happy. For Jacob, building the little world of felt and stone in his basement, it felt like the only adult he could understand.

Not his father, who had wanted career so bad that he had allowed his marriage to collapse. Not his mother, who had wanted marriage so bad that she had become bitter as soon as she realized her husband had never cared for it. Not any of his teachers, or people playing sports, or actors or any of the things that regular kids seemed to want. What he wanted, hard for that one childhood year, and soft for every year after, was to live in the twenty minutes Michael Collins had on the opposite side of the moon. Alone. Silent. Closest to the sun.

The walk home after leaving Daisy was close. If the main street was quiet, the side street back to the shared house was quieter. He relished it, if for no other reason than that his roommates would be awake and loud. Of that, there could be no doubt. And so, he hopped up onto the curb himself, slowed, and listened to the croaking of the frogs.

He would have missed this. The frogs. Sometimes, on nights like this one, after a few beers and warmed with just enough time with Daisy and Christine, he wondered if he could make his home somewhere deeper into the woods. Somewhere where the nature was louder, and the people were quieter.

He wasn’t sure if he’d like it, but he wondered anyway. The frogs had always seemed good company. Not fickle, at least. It was easy to count on a croak when darkness fell.

He stayed up on the shoulder until he reached the next turn off. Then, he pad down the poorly paved road, not even bothering with the shoulder. Any car he would hear from a mile away, and he’d feel the heat of their headlights to boot.

When he arrived at the house, which offered it’s own light, two sets of dusty windows stacked one over the other, he went around the back. And, before he went to bed, he lay out on the deck, the wood still damp from the rain, and listened to the frogs croaking.

 

He didn’t know when he woke. The back of his jacket had soaked through, leaving his skin damp. And there was the buzzing. Buzzing, and the soft mist of rain on his face. Buzzing and buzzing and buzzing, and Jacob could not figure out from where. The frogs were gone. Buzzing buzzing buzzing. It vibrated the wood next to him.

When his mind caught up and he turned, blinking to catch up, he found that his phone was face up. The number was unfamiliar, but he opened it anyway.

Hello, the voice on the other end said. This is University Memorial Hospital.

Everything after the words was a jumble. Jacob’s mind, still half asleep, scrambled to catch.

Hit and run. Surgery. Emergency contact?

“I’m sorry?” croaked Jacob.

“You are Jacob Ballast, are you not?”

“Hit and run?” He caught that, and then the other part. “Who's going into surgery?” And then, finally, his brain processed the name that had come at the very start of the call. "Daisy?"

    A mysterious sequence of impossible robberies.

    The thief, Lydia Mistfarthing, an impossible woman.

    And the detective who aims to get to the bottom of it.

    Prologue

    His heart hammered in his chest as he turned the corner as fast as he could. A blur in the corridor ahead of him, convinced him that he had chosen the right door to go through. After all these months of chasing, he was finally getting close. At the end of the corridor, after another turn, was a series of steps, heading up to the roof. A faint clang sounded from above. “Gotcha” he thought with a satisfied nod.

    Emerging onto the roof, the cold harsh light of day hurt his eyes for a second before they readjusted, letting him see his target in front of him at the edge of the roof. They were facing away from him, their body obscured by a large cloak that fluttered in the breeze. He pointed his crossbow at them, slowly moving forwards. Clearing his throat he announces his presence. “I’m with the Watch. I’ve got you now, no sudden movements”.

    The figure turned, and lowered their hood, revealing a thick mane of copper, wavy hair, and a face obscured by a mask. Despite the mask, her feminine nature was apparent. “Huh” he thought to himself. It wasn’t unheard of for women to be part of the criminal underworld, but it was enough of a rarity that it put him on his guard. 

    After what seemed like an age, trapped in this moment, she finally spoke a single word: “Please”.

    He jabbed his crossbow in her direction again, spurring her on.

    “Please, you have to help me.”

    At this he laughed. “I’ve been chasing you for months, from town to town trying to stop you and make you pay for what you’ve done. Why on earth would I help you?” With each sentence he took a step forward, moving closer to her and the edge of the roof behind her.

    “It’s not what you think, you’ve got it all wrong! You have to help me before he comes back!” she implored. With his closer perspective, he could see it in her posture. He looked again at the shifting of her cloak, he now realised it wasn’t the wind; she was shaking in fear.

    “Explain.” he thundered. “Because from my perspective, I’ve caught you red handed stealing this town’s sacred artefact. And this is hardly the first. I’ve been following your trail for months. The amount you’ve stolen must be worth thousands of pieces at this point.”

    “That’s not the whole story, I didn’t want to do it!” she cried out. The anguish in her voice sounded real to him, but he kept his crossbow trained on her all the same.

    Louder this time, he exclaimed again, “Explain!”, puncturing each syllable with a jab of the crossbow. 

    “It’s a long story, I don’t have the time!” she wailed, getting increasingly agitated. “He’ll be back soon and then it’ll be too late! You have to trust me. Please let me go and I’ll find you later, I promise.”

    By this point, with his slow, methodical steps, he was now in arms reach of her. He grabbed her arm, dragging her away from the edge, towards the door they’d both emerged from earlier. As part of the doorframe, there was an upright beam, and this is what he aimed for, shoving her arm next to it, grabbing the handcuffs from his belt, and quickly securing her to the frame. Target apprehended, he stepped back, lowered his crossbow, and took a seat on a nearby crate. “Now we’ve got all the time in the world, so talk. Tell me everything.”

    Through the mask covering her face, he swore he could almost see tears. “It’s too late she whispered, “He’s here.”. She slid to the floor, as if all the wind was taken from her sails. “I’m sorry”, she breathed, so quiet he could barely hear her.

    And then there was silence. He couldn’t even see her chest move up and down. Was she dead? He got up and slowly walked towards her prone figure, keeping the crossbow targeted at her in case this was a trick. Reaching her, he knelt down and reached past her glove, trying to search for a pulse. Her arm was deathly cold, smooth almost like porcelain, and he couldn’t find a trace of a pulse. Dead then. He uncocked his crossbow and placed it on the floor. Was that it? He leant in, placed his ear to her chest, hoping for any sound of a heart beating. She couldn’t be dead, this was his only lead! 

    There was nothing. Silence. He stepped back, cursing his luck. He turned around and started to examine the roof, trying to see if she left any clues over there, any possible lead for him to follow. There was nothing, no signs that she’d even been there, except her cold dead corpse manacled to the door frame. He wandered back over to it, and kicked the doorframe in his frustration. Which was a stupid thing to do really, as he now hurt his foot, as well as hurting his career by losing a lead. 

    Cursing his misfortune, he was interrupted by the sound of laughter. A small guffaw, with the speaker catching themselves at the end as if they weren’t supposed to be making noise. He turned around, and to his surprise, the woman was standing up again. However something wasn’t right.

    She was standing differently, her posture more assured. Her head was cocked unnaturally to the side, as if analysing him like a bird of prey. And her eyes. Beneath the mask her eyes glowed. He stared.

    “It messed up this time” she said, in a monotone voice, completely unlike what she’d used before. “It was supposed to get in and out with no one noticing, but clearly it messed up. It will have to be punished later for this.”

    He watched as she looked down at the handcuffs still connecting her to the door frame. “Oh you brought handcuffs. How… intriguing. It might have even have enjoyed this in other scenarios.”

    He couldn’t move. His eyes were glued to hers. What witchcraft was this? Reanimation? Possession? 

    “It should really get going. This is boring, and it has to atone for its mistakes.” There was a loud click, and she started to walk back towards the edge of the roof. He looked in shock, as left behind in the handcuff was her hand. He looked back at her as she reached the edge. “You can keep that”, she continued. “Something to remember it by.” 

    She turned around, and he saw the tiniest flicker in her glowing eyes before she whispered, back in the voice she’d used before: “Please, save me.”

    With that she leaned back, and disappeared from his view. He ran to the edge of the roof, expecting to hear the thud of her body hitting the ground, but as he peered over, he saw nothing. She was gone, not a trace of her remained.

    Well that wasn’t entirely true. As if in a trance, he turned around and focused on the body part she’d left behind. He picked it up. It wasn’t a real hand, he was right earlier when he felt porcelain. It was almost like the hand of a doll, scaled up to human size. He ran his fingers over her palm as he felt how smooth it was.
