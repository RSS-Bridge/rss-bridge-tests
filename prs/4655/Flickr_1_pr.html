    • Order in the Mist - Hawke's Bay, NZ

    by: Jos Buurmans


    In recent years, I found myself growing restless with simply capturing the classic scenic views — even in a place as beautiful as Te Mata Peak. I knew the compositions too well, had shot them too many times, and the excitement began to fade. What once felt satisfying started to feel repetitive. So I began looking for something different — not a new location, but a new way of seeing. That shift led me toward a more interpretive style of photography — one focused less on documenting the landscape and more on expressing the mood within it. I became drawn to atmosphere, to form, to the subtle language of light and shadow. These fog-filled mornings offer the perfect canvas for that. Instead of asking, "How do I show this place?" I now ask, "How does this place feel?" And how can I express that through composition, light, and tone? This shift has changed how I approach everything — from when I shoot, to what lens I choose, to how I frame a scene, to how I process the final image. It’s become less about the ‘where’, and more about the why. This image was one of those moments that stops you in your tracks. A double row of trees revealed itself, almost like a page from a storybook — the formal rhythm of trunks, one tier above the next, fading gently into atmospheric perspective. Compositionally, this image relies on symmetry and vertical echoes. I placed the lowest row along the bottom third and let the repeating forms draw the eye upward into the fog. Shooting into the light helped soften outlines and create depth. Post-processing focused on enhancing separation without losing that dreamy softness that mist brings.     • Kaibab Burning

    by: Kirk Lougheed


    Grand Canyon, Arizona Sunset on the Dragon Bravo fire burning on the Kaibab Plateau along the north rim of the Grand Canyon. The low light is catching and reddening the plumes of smoke over 10 miles (16 kilometers) distant from our safe and smokeless location on the canyon’s south rim. The fire was started by lightning on July 4, 2025. As of this posting, it has burned relentlessly for a month aided by very low moisture and high winds, consuming over 116,000 acres (47,00 hectares) of forest and is 12% contained. The north rim of the park is closed for the season. The Grand Canyon Lodge on the north rim has burned. The south rim is open, intact, and has good air quality, thanks to prevailing southwesterly winds. Explored August 4, 2025

    • Follow the Light

    by: Elizabeth Gadd


    A self portrait taken in the Canadian prairies as the sky lit up with beautiful, dancing aurora. My partner and I spent the night camping in our vehicle with hopes that the aurora would show up that night - and at around 1:30am, it did! I happened to have a candle in my bag from a previous shoot, which ended up being the best addition I could imagine for this image, taking it to the next level... holding onto and looking into the light, while the nighttime sky swirled and danced all around me. I wanted this image to portray the idea of always looking for the light in life, and how even the darkest days can make the light seem even brighter. Fun fact: It was -10 Celsius and super windy this night, making it extremely difficult to actually capture this peaceful looking photo! Every time my partner lit the candle (my fingers were too cold to light it myself, haha), it blew out in the wind as soon as I got into position... it took a good couple solid hours of attempts before everything finally lined up for this shot. I was so frozen, but in the end it was worth the cold and exhilaration!
